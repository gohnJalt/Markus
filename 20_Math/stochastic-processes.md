# Stochastic Processes

This file is the direct sequel to [[`probability.md`]] and the
bridge between the foundational math layer and the time series
and structural macro work that Markus actually does. [[`probability.md`]]
built the limit theorems for sequences of i.i.d. or weakly
dependent random variables: LLNs, CLTs, the delta method,
$o_P/O_P$ notation, Prokhorov, and Donsker stated. This file
builds the *processes* — random quantities that evolve in time
— and the tools for reasoning about them.

The results that matter most for econ work are: the martingale
theory that underlies GMM asymptotics in time series and the
proof of the martingale difference CLT (§2); stationarity,
ergodicity, and mixing, which are the conditions under which
LLNs and CLTs extend from i.i.d. data to time series (§3);
the Wold decomposition, which explains why autoregressive
models work at all (§4); ARMA processes and their state-space
representations, which are the workhorses of empirical macro
(§5); Brownian motion and Itô calculus, which are the
continuous-time limits that cointegration tests, optimal
stopping problems, and continuous-time structural models rest
on (§§6-7); and unit root asymptotics with the Dickey-Fuller
distribution as a functional of Brownian motion, which is where
the local-to-unity framework lives (§8). Section 9 covers
Markov chains at the working level that [[`dynamic-programming.md`]]
needs.

Prerequisites: [[`probability.md`]] (the full file, especially
§§3-6 on CLTs, weak convergence, and Donsker), [[`measure-theory.md`]]
(§§1-4 for the integral and modes of convergence), and
[[`functional-analysis.md`]] (§§2-3 for Hilbert spaces and the
projection theorem — the Wold decomposition is a projection
argument). [[`linear-algebra.md`]] §3.4 (VAR stability via
eigenvalues of the companion matrix) and §5 (SVD) are useful
background for the ARMA and state-space sections.

## 1. Filtrations and adapted processes

### 1.1 The framework

The probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ from
[[`measure-theory.md`]] §8 is a single static object. To model
a system that evolves in time, we need to add structure that
tracks what information is available at each point in time.

A **filtration** is an increasing family $\mathbb{F} =
(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t \in T}$ of sub-$\sigma$-algebras of
$\mathcal{F}$: $\mathcal{F}_s \subseteq \mathcal{F}_t
\subseteq \mathcal{F}$ for $s \leq t$. Think of
$\mathcal{F}_t$ as the collection of all events that can
be determined by observing the system up through time $t$.
In discrete time, $T = \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$; in continuous
time, $T = [0, \infty)$.

A stochastic process $(X_t)_{t \in T}$ is **adapted** to
$\mathbb{F}$ if $X_t$ is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable for each
$t$ — i.e., the value of $X_t$ is knowable given information
available at time $t$. Every sensible time-series model is an
adapted process. An adapted process is **predictable** if
$X_t$ is $\mathcal{F}_{t-1}$-measurable — i.e., the value
of $X_t$ is determined by the information available *before*
time $t$.

**The natural filtration.** Given a process $(X_t)$, the
*natural filtration* $\mathcal{F}_t^X = \sigma(X_s : s \leq t)$
is the filtration generated by the process itself — the
smallest filtration to which the process is adapted.

### 1.2 Conditional expectation in the filtration

The conditional expectation $E[X | \mathcal{F}_t]$ is the
$L^2$ projection of $X$ onto the closed subspace of
$\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable functions, as developed in
[[`functional-analysis.md`]] §9 and [[`measure-theory.md`]] §9. In
the filtration context:

- $E[X | \mathcal{F}_s] = E[E[X|\mathcal{F}_t]|\mathcal{F}_s]$
  for $s \leq t$ — the **tower property**. Larger information
  sets nest: conditioning first on more and then on less gives
  the same answer as conditioning directly on less.
- If $X$ is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable, then
  $E[X|\mathcal{F}_t] = X$ a.s. — you already know the value.
- If $X \perp \mathcal{F}_t$, then $E[X|\mathcal{F}_t] =
  E[X]$ a.s. — the information is irrelevant.

The tower property is the algebraic engine of martingale
theory and dynamic programming alike.

## 2. Martingale theory

### 2.1 Definitions

A sequence $(M_t, \mathcal{F}_t)_{t \geq 0}$ is a
**martingale** if, for each $t$:
1. $M_t$ is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable (adapted),
2. $E[|M_t|] < \infty$ ($L^1$-bounded),
3. $E[M_{t+1} | \mathcal{F}_t] = M_t$ a.s. (the martingale property).

Condition 3 says that the best forecast of the next value,
given current information, is the current value. A martingale
is a fair game: no strategy using current information
generates a predictable gain. A **submartingale** replaces
the equality with $\geq$; a **supermartingale** with $\leq$.

**Standard examples.** (i) Cumulative sums of zero-mean i.i.d.
variables: $M_n = \sum_{i=1}^n \xi_i$ with $E[\xi_i] = 0$,
$\mathcal{F}_n = \sigma(\xi_1, \ldots, \xi_n)$. The increment
$M_{n+1} - M_n = \xi_{n+1}$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_n$
with zero mean. (ii) The likelihood ratio process:
$M_n = \prod_{i=1}^n (p(X_i)/q(X_i))$ under measure $Q$
where the density of $P$ with respect to $Q$ is
$\prod p/q$. (iii) $E[X | \mathcal{F}_t]$ for any integrable
$X$ — this is the fundamental example, since the tower
property gives $E[E[X|\mathcal{F}_{t+1}]|\mathcal{F}_t] =
E[X|\mathcal{F}_t]$.

### 2.2 Doob's decomposition

**Theorem (Doob decomposition).** Every adapted integrable
sequence $(X_t)$ can be written uniquely as
$$X_t = M_t + A_t,$$
where $(M_t)$ is a martingale and $(A_t)$ is a predictable
process (i.e., $A_t$ is $\mathcal{F}_{t-1}$-measurable) with
$A_0 = 0$.

**Proof.** Set $A_0 = 0$ and define the increments
$$A_t - A_{t-1} = E[X_t | \mathcal{F}_{t-1}] - X_{t-1},$$
so $A_t = \sum_{s=1}^t (E[X_s|\mathcal{F}_{s-1}] - X_{s-1})$.
This is $\mathcal{F}_{t-1}$-measurable by definition, hence
predictable. Set $M_t = X_t - A_t$. Then
$$E[M_t | \mathcal{F}_{t-1}] = E[X_t | \mathcal{F}_{t-1}]
- A_t = E[X_t|\mathcal{F}_{t-1}] - E[X_t|\mathcal{F}_{t-1}]
+ X_{t-1} = M_{t-1}.$$
So $(M_t)$ is a martingale. Uniqueness follows because if
$X_t = M_t + A_t = M_t' + A_t'$, then
$M_t - M_t' = A_t' - A_t$ is both a martingale and
predictable, which forces it to be identically zero. $\blacksquare$

The Doob decomposition is the discrete analogue of the
semimartingale decomposition used in stochastic calculus. In
econometrics: the Beveridge-Nelson decomposition of an I(1)
process into a random walk plus a stationary component is
a Doob decomposition; so is the decomposition of a sum
of correlated variables into a martingale plus a remainder
that the CLT argument controls.

### 2.3 Martingale convergence

**Theorem (Doob's $L^2$ martingale convergence).** If $(M_t)$
is a martingale with $\sup_t E[M_t^2] < \infty$ (an
$L^2$-bounded martingale), then there exists $M_\infty \in L^2$
such that $M_t \to M_\infty$ both almost surely and in $L^2$.

**Proof sketch.** The key tool is **Doob's upcrossing
inequality**: the expected number of times $(M_t)$ crosses
the interval $[a, b]$ from below is bounded by
$(E[|M_T - a|] - E[(M_0 - a)^+]) / (b - a)$, which is
finite for each $T$ and bounded as $T \to \infty$ under
$L^2$ boundedness (by Jensen: $E[|M_T|] \leq \sqrt{E[M_T^2]}
\leq C$). A sequence of real numbers converges if and only if
it has finitely many upcrossings of every interval $[a,b]$
with rational endpoints. The upcrossing inequality
guarantees this a.s., giving $M_t \to M_\infty$ a.s. by a
Borel-Cantelli argument. $L^2$ convergence then follows from
the $L^2$ boundedness and the dominated convergence theorem
(since $|M_t - M_\infty|^2 \leq 4\sup_t M_t^2$ and the
bound is in $L^1$ when $\sup E[M_t^2] < \infty$, after a
standard localization). $\blacksquare$

### 2.4 The martingale difference CLT

The most important CLT for time-series econometrics: the
central limit theorem for martingale difference sequences
(MDS). This is the result deferred from [[`probability.md`]] §3.4.

**Definition.** A sequence $(\xi_t, \mathcal{F}_t)$ is a
**martingale difference sequence** if $\xi_t$ is
$\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable and $E[\xi_t | \mathcal{F}_{t-1}]
= 0$ a.s. for all $t$.

The differences of a martingale form an MDS, but the
definition is broader: an MDS can have arbitrary conditional
heteroskedasticity.

**Theorem (MDS CLT, McLeish 1974).** Let $(\xi_{n,t})$ be a
triangular array that is an MDS with respect to
$(\mathcal{F}_{n,t})$. Suppose:
1. $\sum_{t=1}^n E[\xi_{n,t}^2|\mathcal{F}_{n,t-1}]
   \xrightarrow{P} \sigma^2 > 0$ (conditional variance
   stabilizes in probability),
2. $\sum_{t=1}^n E[\xi_{n,t}^2 \mathbf{1}(|\xi_{n,t}|
   > \varepsilon)|\mathcal{F}_{n,t-1}] \xrightarrow{P} 0$
   for all $\varepsilon > 0$ (conditional Lindeberg condition).

Then $\sum_{t=1}^n \xi_{n,t} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$.

**Why this matters.** GMM in time series sets up the estimating
equations as a sample average of moment conditions
$g_t(\theta) = m(X_t, \theta) - E[m(X_t,\theta)]$.
Under the null, these are an MDS (or are close to one after
prewhitening). The MDS CLT gives asymptotic normality of the
GMM estimator; the sandwich variance formula carries the HAC
structure. OLS with time-series errors falls under the same
framework: the score $X_t \varepsilon_t$ is an MDS if
$E[\varepsilon_t | X_t, X_{t-1}, \ldots] = 0$ (strict
exogeneity in time series). Without that, the score is merely
a weakly dependent process and Gordin's condition applies
(§3.3 below).

## 3. Stationarity, ergodicity, and mixing

### 3.1 Stationarity

A stochastic process $(X_t)_{t \in \mathbb{Z}}$ is
**strictly stationary** if for every $k \geq 1$, every
$t_1 < \cdots < t_k$, and every $h \in \mathbb{Z}$:
$$(X_{t_1}, \ldots, X_{t_k}) \stackrel{d}{=}
(X_{t_1+h}, \ldots, X_{t_k+h}).$$
The joint distribution is invariant to time shifts.

A process is **weakly (or covariance-) stationary** if:
1. $E[X_t] = \mu$ for all $t$ (constant mean),
2. $\mathrm{Cov}(X_t, X_{t+h}) = \gamma(h)$ for all $t$
   (the autocovariance depends only on the lag $h$, not $t$).

Strict stationarity with finite second moments implies weak
stationarity. Weak stationarity does not imply strict: a
GARCH process is weakly stationary (constant unconditional
variance) but not strictly stationary if the distribution
of the innovations changes with the state.

The **autocovariance function** $\gamma(h)$ and the
**autocorrelation function** $\rho(h) = \gamma(h)/\gamma(0)$
summarize the linear dependence structure. They satisfy
$\gamma(h) = \gamma(-h)$ (symmetry) and
$|\gamma(h)| \leq \gamma(0)$ (bounded by variance). A
covariance-stationary process has an absolutely summable
autocovariance function if $\sum_{h=-\infty}^\infty
|\gamma(h)| < \infty$ — this is the *short-memory* condition.

### 3.2 Ergodicity

Stationarity alone does not give a law of large numbers:
a process could be stationary but have all its mass on
a single non-random trajectory (a degenerate case where
the time average equals the path, not the mean). Ergodicity
rules this out.

Formally: an event $A \in \mathcal{F}$ is **invariant** under
the time-shift operator $\tau$ (which maps $\omega \mapsto$
the shifted sequence) if $\tau^{-1}(A) = A$. A stationary
process is **ergodic** if every invariant event has probability
0 or 1.

**Theorem (Ergodic theorem, Birkhoff 1931).** If $(X_t)$ is
strictly stationary and ergodic with $E[|X_0|] < \infty$,
then
$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^n X_t \xrightarrow{a.s.} E[X_0].$$

This is the LLN for stationary ergodic processes. It holds
under strict stationarity plus ergodicity — no independence,
no mixing rate condition. The cost is that the CLT requires
more: either a rate of mixing or the MDS structure.

**Standard examples.** i.i.d. sequences are ergodic (trivially:
a shifted i.i.d. sequence has the same distribution, and
independence makes all invariant sets trivial). AR(1) with
$|\phi| < 1$ driven by i.i.d. innovations is ergodic.
A process with $X_t = X_0$ for all $t$ (all paths constant)
is stationary but not ergodic: the event $\{X_0 > 0\}$ is
invariant and has probability $P(X_0 > 0) \in (0, 1)$ if
$X_0$ is non-degenerate.

### 3.3 Mixing conditions and Gordin's CLT

For a CLT, ergodicity alone is not enough — the process
must "forget" its past fast enough. Mixing conditions
formalize this.

**$\alpha$-mixing (strong mixing).** Define the mixing
coefficient
$$\alpha(k) = \sup_{A \in \mathcal{F}_{-\infty}^0,\,
B \in \mathcal{F}_k^\infty} |P(A \cap B) - P(A)P(B)|.$$
The process is **$\alpha$-mixing** if $\alpha(k) \to 0$
as $k \to \infty$. This is the weakest standard mixing
condition: distant events become nearly independent.

**$\beta$-mixing** and **$\phi$-mixing** are stronger
($\phi$-mixing is the strongest; $\phi$-mixing implies
$\beta$-mixing implies $\alpha$-mixing).

**CLT under mixing (Gordin 1969).** Suppose $(X_t)$ is
stationary and ergodic with $E[X_0] = 0$ and $E[X_0^2]
< \infty$. The **long-run variance** is
$$\sigma^2_{\mathrm{LR}} = \sum_{h=-\infty}^\infty \gamma(h)
= \gamma(0) + 2\sum_{h=1}^\infty \gamma(h),$$
provided the sum converges absolutely (the short-memory
condition). Gordin's condition requires that the process
admits a decomposition $X_t = M_t + (Z_{t-1} - Z_t)$, where
$(M_t)$ is an $L^2$-bounded MDS and $Z_t \in L^2$. Under
this condition,
$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{t=1}^n X_t \xrightarrow{d}
\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_{\mathrm{LR}}).$$

Gordin's condition is implied by $\alpha$-mixing with
$\sum_k \alpha(k)^{1-2/r} < \infty$ for some $r > 2$
(Ibragimov 1962). Many standard time-series models (stable
AR and ARMA, GARCH with appropriate moment conditions) are
geometrically $\alpha$-mixing and so satisfy Gordin's
condition automatically.

**Why this matters for OLS and GMM.** In time series, OLS
residuals are not i.i.d. The variance of the OLS estimator
involves the long-run variance of the score $X_t\varepsilon_t$,
not just its contemporaneous variance. HAC (heteroskedasticity-
and-autocorrelation-consistent) standard errors estimate
$\sigma^2_{\mathrm{LR}}$. The Gordin CLT is the mathematical
justification that they work. Newey-West, Andrews, and other
HAC estimators are estimating the spectral density of
$X_t\varepsilon_t$ at frequency zero — which is exactly
$\sigma^2_{\mathrm{LR}} / (2\pi)$.

## 4. The Wold decomposition

**Theorem (Wold 1938).** Let $(X_t)$ be a covariance-
stationary process with zero mean. Then $X_t$ admits a
unique decomposition
$$X_t = \sum_{j=0}^\infty \psi_j \varepsilon_{t-j} + V_t,$$
where:
1. $\varepsilon_t = X_t - P(X_t | \overline{\text{sp}}\{X_{t-1},
   X_{t-2}, \ldots\})$ is the one-step-ahead linear prediction
   error — a white noise sequence ($E[\varepsilon_t] = 0$,
   $E[\varepsilon_t \varepsilon_s] = 0$ for $t \neq s$,
   $E[\varepsilon_t^2] = \sigma^2$).
2. $\psi_0 = 1$ and $\sum_{j=0}^\infty \psi_j^2 < \infty$.
3. $V_t$ is a *linearly deterministic* component — perfectly
   predictable from its own linear past, with $E[V_t \varepsilon_s]
   = 0$ for all $t, s$.

**Proof sketch (Hilbert space argument).** Work in the Hilbert
space $H = \overline{\text{sp}}\{X_t : t \in \mathbb{Z}\}$
with inner product $\langle X_t, X_s \rangle = E[X_t X_s] =
\gamma(t-s)$. Define the linear past at time $t$ as
$H_t = \overline{\text{sp}}\{X_s : s \leq t\}$. The
projection theorem ([[`functional-analysis.md`]] §3.1) gives a
unique decomposition of $X_t$ into its projection onto
$H_{t-1}$ (the predictable part) and the orthogonal
complement (the innovation $\varepsilon_t = X_t -
P_{H_{t-1}} X_t$). The innovations are orthogonal by
construction. The deterministic subspace $H_{-\infty} =
\bigcap_{t} H_t$ (the far past) is a closed subspace of $H$;
$V_t = P_{H_{-\infty}} X_t$ is the projection onto it. The
moving-average coefficients $\psi_j$ emerge from iterating
the projection. $\blacksquare$

**What the Wold decomposition means.** Every covariance-
stationary process without a deterministic component is an
infinite-order moving average of its own innovations.
This is the structural reason ARMA approximations work:
any MA($\infty$) with square-summable coefficients can
be approximated by a finite-order ARMA model (the key
approximation theorem in time-series analysis). The Wold
representation is *constructive* in the sense that
$\varepsilon_t$ is the actual one-step-ahead forecast error —
it is the innovation in a deep sense, not just a notational
device.

**The role of invertibility.** Not every MA($\infty$)
representation is invertible — i.e., can be written as an
AR($\infty$). Invertibility requires that the MA polynomial
has all roots outside the unit circle (equivalently,
$\sum |\psi_j| z^j \neq 0$ for $|z| \leq 1$). Invertibility
matters because estimation of the Wold representation
coefficients in practice requires inverting the MA filter;
if it is non-invertible, the autoregression diverges.

## 5. ARMA processes and state-space representations

### 5.1 The ARMA family

An **ARMA(p, q)** process satisfies
$$X_t - \phi_1 X_{t-1} - \cdots - \phi_p X_{t-p} =
\varepsilon_t + \theta_1 \varepsilon_{t-1} + \cdots +
\theta_q \varepsilon_{t-q},$$
where $(\varepsilon_t)$ is white noise with variance
$\sigma^2$. In lag-operator notation: $\Phi(L) X_t =
\Theta(L) \varepsilon_t$, where $\Phi(z) = 1 - \phi_1 z -
\cdots - \phi_p z^p$ and $\Theta(z) = 1 + \theta_1 z +
\cdots + \theta_q z^q$.

**Stationarity condition:** $\Phi(z) \neq 0$ for all
$|z| \leq 1$ — the AR polynomial has all roots strictly
outside the unit circle.

**Invertibility condition:** $\Theta(z) \neq 0$ for all
$|z| \leq 1$ — the MA polynomial has all roots strictly
outside the unit circle. Under invertibility, the
ARMA(p,q) has an AR($\infty$) representation:
$\Pi(L) X_t = \varepsilon_t$ where $\Pi(z) = \Phi(z)/\Theta(z)$.

**Spectral density.** A covariance-stationary process has
a spectral representation via the Herglotz theorem: there
exists a nondecreasing function $F$ (the spectral distribution
function) such that
$$\gamma(h) = \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{i\omega h} dF(\omega).$$
If $F$ is absolutely continuous, the density $f(\omega) =
F'(\omega)$ is the **spectral density**. For an ARMA(p,q):
$$f(\omega) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\pi}
\frac{|\Theta(e^{i\omega})|^2}{|\Phi(e^{i\omega})|^2}.$$
The spectral density at frequency zero, $f(0) =
\sigma^2_{LR} / (2\pi)$, is the long-run variance (connecting
to §3.3). HAC estimators are kernel estimators of $f(0)$.

### 5.2 Vector ARMA and VAR processes

The **VAR(p)** in $n$ variables is
$$\mathbf{y}_t = A_1 \mathbf{y}_{t-1} + \cdots + A_p
\mathbf{y}_{t-p} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t,$$
with $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t \sim (0, \Sigma)$ white noise.
The **companion form** writes the VAR as a first-order system:
$$\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{y}_t \\
\mathbf{y}_{t-1} \\ \vdots \\ \mathbf{y}_{t-p+1}
\end{pmatrix}}_{\mathbf{Y}_t} =
\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} A_1 & A_2 & \cdots & A_p \\
I & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \ddots & & \vdots \\
0 & \cdots & I & 0 \end{pmatrix}}_{C}
\mathbf{Y}_{t-1} + \begin{pmatrix} \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_t \\
\mathbf{0} \\ \vdots \\ \mathbf{0} \end{pmatrix}.$$

Stability (covariance-stationarity) requires all eigenvalues
of the companion matrix $C$ to satisfy $|\lambda_i| < 1$ —
i.e., spectral radius $\rho(C) < 1$ ([[`linear-algebra.md`]] §3.4).
The impulse response function at horizon $h$ is the $(1,1)$
block of $C^h$; this is computable via matrix exponentiation
and is the object that local projections also estimate
([[`local-projections.md`]]).

### 5.3 State-space representation

Every ARMA(p,q) admits a **state-space representation**:
$$\mathbf{s}_t = F \mathbf{s}_{t-1} + G \varepsilon_t
\quad \text{(state equation)},$$
$$X_t = H \mathbf{s}_t + v_t
\quad \text{(observation equation)},$$
where $\mathbf{s}_t$ is a $k$-dimensional state vector
(possibly unobserved), $F$ is the transition matrix, $G$
maps innovations to states, $H$ extracts the observable,
and $v_t$ is measurement noise (which may be zero).

For an ARMA(p,q): take $k = \max(p, q+1)$, state
$\mathbf{s}_t = (X_t, X_{t+1|t}, \ldots, X_{t+k-1|t})$
where $X_{t+j|t}$ is the $j$-step-ahead linear forecast,
$F$ is the companion-type matrix, $H = (1, 0, \ldots, 0)$.

**The Kalman filter** is the recursive algorithm for computing
$E[\mathbf{s}_t | X_1, \ldots, X_t]$ and its conditional
covariance $P_t = \mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{s}_t | X_1, \ldots, X_t)$,
given state-space parameters. The recursion alternates a
prediction step and an update step:
$$\mathbf{s}_{t|t-1} = F \mathbf{s}_{t-1|t-1}, \quad
P_{t|t-1} = F P_{t-1|t-1} F^\top + G G^\top \sigma^2,$$
$$K_t = P_{t|t-1} H^\top (H P_{t|t-1} H^\top + \sigma_v^2)^{-1}
\quad \text{(Kalman gain)},$$
$$\mathbf{s}_{t|t} = \mathbf{s}_{t|t-1} + K_t(X_t - H
\mathbf{s}_{t|t-1}), \quad P_{t|t} = (I - K_t H)P_{t|t-1}.$$
The prediction error $X_t - H\mathbf{s}_{t|t-1}$ is a
martingale difference sequence, and its squared norm (the
innovation variance $H P_{t|t-1} H^\top + \sigma_v^2$) is
the denominator in the Kalman gain. State-space estimation
via maximum likelihood optimizes the Gaussian likelihood
written in terms of these prediction errors (the *prediction-
error decomposition*); Cholesky on the innovation covariance
([[`linear-algebra.md`]] §7) makes the computation stable.

## 6. Brownian motion

### 6.1 Definition and existence

A **standard Brownian motion** (Wiener process)
$W = (W_t)_{t \geq 0}$ is a stochastic process satisfying:
1. $W_0 = 0$ a.s.
2. **Independent increments**: for $0 \leq s < t$,
   $W_t - W_s$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_s^W =
   \sigma(W_u : u \leq s)$.
3. **Gaussian increments**: $W_t - W_s \sim \mathcal{N}(0, t-s)$.
4. **Continuous paths**: $t \mapsto W_t$ is continuous a.s.

Properties 1-3 determine the finite-dimensional distributions
uniquely: by properties 2 and 3, the joint distribution of
$(W_{t_1}, W_{t_2} - W_{t_1}, \ldots, W_{t_k} - W_{t_{k-1}})$
is a multivariate Gaussian with independent components. The
Kolmogorov extension theorem ([[`measure-theory.md`]] §6) gives
a probability space on which such a consistent family of
finite-dimensional distributions exists; property 4 is
then established by Kolmogorov's continuity criterion (the
tightness condition $E[|W_t - W_s|^\alpha] \leq C|t-s|^{1+\beta}$
is satisfied for $\alpha = 4$, $\beta = 1$, $C = 3$, using
the Gaussian fourth moment).

**Donsker's theorem as construction.** The constructive
existence proof of Brownian motion is exactly Donsker's
theorem ([[`probability.md`]] §6.3): the normalized partial-sum
process of i.i.d. mean-zero variance-1 random variables
converges weakly to Brownian motion in $C[0,1]$. Donsker is
both a CLT for the path as a whole and an existence proof for
the Wiener measure.

### 6.2 Key properties

**Non-differentiability.** $W$ is a.s. nowhere differentiable.
The heuristic: $W_t - W_s \sim \mathcal{N}(0, t-s)$, so
$(W_t - W_s)/(t-s) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, (t-s)^{-1}) \to \infty$
as $t \to s$. A rigorous argument uses the Borel-Cantelli
lemma on the fluctuations.

**Quadratic variation.** Along any sequence of partitions
$0 = t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_n = T$ with mesh $\to 0$:
$$\sum_{k=1}^n (W_{t_k} - W_{t_{k-1}})^2 \xrightarrow{L^2} T.$$
We write $[W, W]_t = t$ — the quadratic variation of
$W$ over $[0, t]$ is $t$ deterministically. This is the
crucial property that makes Brownian motion the natural
integrator in stochastic calculus. It also means Brownian
motion has infinite variation on every interval, which is
why Riemann-Stieltjes integration cannot define $\int f\, dW$
— a new integral is needed.

**Scaling.** For any $c > 0$, $(c^{-1/2} W_{ct})_{t \geq 0}$
is also a standard Brownian motion. This self-similarity
(scale-invariance) means no time scale is distinguished —
Brownian motion looks the same at every resolution. This
property distinguishes Brownian motion from fractional
Brownian motion (§8 below), where the exponent differs from
$1/2$.

**Reflection principle.** $P(\max_{s \leq t} W_s \geq a)
= 2P(W_t \geq a)$ for $a > 0$. This gives the distribution
of the running maximum and the Brownian bridge, and underlies
the Kolmogorov-Smirnov distribution.

## 7. Itô calculus

### 7.1 The Itô integral

Because Brownian motion has infinite variation, the
Riemann-Stieltjes integral $\int_0^T f(t)\, dW_t$ cannot
be defined pathwise. The **Itô integral** defines it
as an $L^2$ limit of simple integrands.

For a simple predictable process $f(t) = \sum_{k} f_k
\mathbf{1}_{(t_k, t_{k+1}]}(t)$ where $f_k$ is
$\mathcal{F}_{t_k}$-measurable, define
$$\int_0^T f(t)\, dW_t = \sum_k f_k (W_{t_{k+1}} - W_{t_k}).$$
This is a sum of $\mathcal{F}_{t_k}$-measurable weights times
independent Gaussian increments — a martingale. The Itô
isometry:
$$E\!\left[\left(\int_0^T f(t)\, dW_t\right)^2\right] =
E\!\left[\int_0^T f(t)^2\, dt\right],$$
shows that the map $f \mapsto \int f\, dW$ is an isometry
from $L^2([0,T] \times \Omega, dt \times P)$ (adapted
processes) to $L^2(\Omega, P)$. The Itô integral of a general
adapted $L^2$ process is the $L^2$ limit of simple integrals.

**The resulting integral is a martingale**: $E[\int_0^t
f\, dW | \mathcal{F}_s] = \int_0^s f\, dW$ for $s \leq t$.
The Itô integral adds no expected gain — it is a fair-game
integral, consistent with the martingale property of Brownian
motion.

### 7.2 Itô's lemma

Itô's lemma is the chain rule of stochastic calculus. Because
Brownian motion has nonzero quadratic variation, the ordinary
chain rule acquires a second-order correction.

**Theorem (Itô's lemma, 1-d).** Let $X_t = X_0 +
\int_0^t \mu_s\, ds + \int_0^t \sigma_s\, dW_s$ be an Itô
process (the SDE shorthand: $dX_t = \mu_t\, dt + \sigma_t\,
dW_t$). Let $f: [0,T] \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be
$C^{1,2}$ (once continuously differentiable in $t$, twice in
$x$). Then $f(t, X_t)$ is also an Itô process, with

$$df(t, X_t) = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} +
\mu_t \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} +
\frac{\sigma_t^2}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}\right)dt
+ \sigma_t \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\, dW_t.$$

**Proof sketch.** Taylor-expand $f(t + dt, X_t + dX_t)$
keeping terms through second order. The new ingredient:
$(dW_t)^2 = dt$ in the $L^2$ sense (the quadratic variation
result from §6.2). Ordinary calculus drops the $(dt)^2$ and
$dt\, dW_t$ terms as higher order; Itô calculus keeps the
$(dW_t)^2 = dt$ term, yielding the second-derivative
correction $(\sigma_t^2/2) \partial^2 f/\partial x^2\, dt$.
$\blacksquare$

**Standard applications.**

- **Geometric Brownian motion.** $dS_t = \mu S_t\, dt +
  \sigma S_t\, dW_t$ with $S_0 > 0$. Apply Itô's lemma to
  $f(t, x) = \log x$: $d\log S_t = (\mu - \sigma^2/2)dt +
  \sigma\, dW_t$. So $\log S_t = \log S_0 + (\mu - \sigma^2/2)t
  + \sigma W_t$, and $S_t = S_0 \exp((\mu - \sigma^2/2)t +
  \sigma W_t)$ is lognormal. The $-\sigma^2/2$ Itô correction
  is the Jensen's inequality term: $E[\log S_t] < \log E[S_t]$.

- **Ornstein-Uhlenbeck.** $dX_t = -\kappa X_t\, dt + \sigma\,
  dW_t$ ($\kappa > 0$). This is the continuous-time AR(1):
  the process reverts to zero at rate $\kappa$. The stationary
  distribution is $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2/(2\kappa))$.
  OU processes appear in models of interest rates (Vasicek),
  labor market dynamics, and the continuous-time limits of
  ARMA models.

- **Dynkin's formula.** For a process $X_t$ with generator
  $\mathcal{A}$ (the drift operator on the right-hand side
  of Itô's lemma, minus the $dW$ term), $E[f(X_T)] =
  f(X_0) + E[\int_0^T \mathcal{A}f(X_t)\, dt]$ for
  suitable $f$. This is the bridge between SDEs and the PDEs
  (Kolmogorov forward/backward equations) that structural
  macro models and option pricing derive from.

## 8. Unit roots and cointegration

### 8.1 The random walk as a degenerate ARMA

A **random walk** is $X_t = X_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$
($\varepsilon_t$ i.i.d. mean zero, variance $\sigma^2$).
This is an ARMA(1,0) with $\phi = 1$ — the AR polynomial
$\Phi(z) = 1 - z$ has a root exactly at $z = 1$, on the
unit circle. The stationarity condition fails.

**Key consequences.** (i) $X_t = X_0 + \sum_{s=1}^t
\varepsilon_s$, so $\mathrm{Var}(X_t) = t\sigma^2 \to \infty$.
(ii) Sample moments diverge: $T^{-1}\sum_{t=1}^T X_t^2 =
O_P(T)$ rather than $O_P(1)$. (iii) OLS on a regression of
$X_t$ on $Y_t$ (an independent random walk) gives $t$-statistics
that diverge at rate $T^{1/2}$ — *spurious regression*
(Granger-Newbold 1974, Phillips 1986). The standard critical
values are useless.

**The Beveridge-Nelson decomposition.** Any I(1) process
$X_t$ satisfying $\Delta X_t = (1-L)X_t =: u_t$ stationary
can be written as
$$X_t = X_0 + \Psi(1) \sum_{s=1}^t \varepsilon_s + v_t - v_0,$$
where $\Psi(1) = \sum_{j=0}^\infty \psi_j$ is the long-run
impact (the sum of Wold coefficients) and $v_t$ is a
covariance-stationary process. The first term is the
*stochastic trend* — a scaled random walk. The Beveridge-Nelson
decomposition is the discrete-time Doob decomposition made
explicit.

### 8.2 Local-to-unity asymptotics

The **local-to-unity** framework places the AR root at
$$\phi_T = 1 - c/T,$$
for fixed $c \geq 0$, so the root approaches 1 as $T \to \infty$.
This parameterization captures the idea that an
"almost-integrated" process — near but not at the unit circle
— should have intermediate behavior between the stationary
and unit-root cases.

Under local-to-unity, the partial-sum process has a
continuous-time limit. Define $X_{\lfloor Tr \rfloor} / \sigma
\sqrt{T}$ for $r \in [0,1]$. As $T \to \infty$, this
converges weakly in $C[0,1]$ to the **Ornstein-Uhlenbeck
diffusion** $J_c(r)$ satisfying
$$dJ_c(r) = -c\, J_c(r)\, dr + dW(r), \quad J_c(0) = 0.$$
When $c = 0$, $J_0(r) = W(r)$: the limit is standard Brownian
motion (the unit-root case). When $c > 0$, $J_c$ is
mean-reverting; the stationary autocovariance structure of the
stationary case re-emerges in the limit.

This framework is the correct way to think about the power
of unit-root tests: any test has power determined by how
far $c$ is from zero, and Gaussian testing is equivalent
to designing tests that maximize weighted power against
$c > 0$.

### 8.3 The Dickey-Fuller test and its distribution

The **Dickey-Fuller test** tests $H_0: \phi = 1$ in
$X_t = \phi X_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$ via the OLS-$t$ statistic
for $\hat\phi - 1 = 0$, or equivalently via the statistic
$T(\hat\phi - 1)$ (which converges faster than $\sqrt{T}$
when $\phi = 1$).

**Theorem (Dickey-Fuller limiting distribution).** Under
$H_0: \phi = 1$:
$$T(\hat\phi - 1) \xrightarrow{d}
\frac{\frac{1}{2}(W(1)^2 - 1)}{\int_0^1 W(r)^2\, dr},$$
$$t_{\hat\phi} \xrightarrow{d}
\frac{\frac{1}{2}(W(1)^2 - 1)}{\left(\int_0^1 W(r)^2\, dr\right)^{1/2}},$$
where $W$ is standard Brownian motion on $[0, 1]$.

These are non-Gaussian distributions (functionals of Brownian
motion) that must be tabulated by simulation. The critical
values are more negative than the standard normal — rejecting
$H_0$ requires a more extreme $t$-statistic than $-1.96$.
The reason: under the null, the regressor $X_{t-1}$ has
standard deviation of order $T^{1/2}$, which is faster than
the $n^{1/2}$ rate in a stationary regression, causing
$T(\hat\phi - 1)$ to converge at rate $T$ rather than $\sqrt{T}$.

**The augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test** extends this to
ARMA errors: include lagged differences $\Delta X_{t-1},
\ldots, \Delta X_{t-p}$ in the regression to whiten the
residuals. The limiting distribution is the same as DF.

**Deterministic terms.** The distribution changes depending
on whether a constant and/or trend is included in the
regression (three standard cases: no deterministic terms;
constant only; constant and trend). The critical values
differ across cases. The choice of deterministic terms
depends on the DGP — including too many inflates the
variance, too few causes test size distortion.

### 8.4 Cointegration

Two I(1) processes $X_t$ and $Y_t$ are **cointegrated** if
there exists $\beta$ such that $Y_t - \beta X_t$ is
$I(0)$ (covariance-stationary). The vector $(1, -\beta)$ is
the *cointegrating vector*. Cointegration formalizes the
idea of a long-run equilibrium relationship: both $X_t$ and
$Y_t$ wander, but they wander together.

**The Granger representation theorem:** If $(X_t, Y_t)$
are cointegrated, there exists an error-correction
representation
$$\Delta X_t = \alpha_X (Y_{t-1} - \beta X_{t-1}) +
\text{lags} + \varepsilon_{X,t},$$
$$\Delta Y_t = \alpha_Y (Y_{t-1} - \beta X_{t-1}) +
\text{lags} + \varepsilon_{Y,t},$$
where $\alpha_X, \alpha_Y$ are error-correction speeds
(at least one is nonzero). The system is pulled back toward
the equilibrium $Y_t = \beta X_t$ whenever it deviates.

**Engle-Granger two-step:** (1) Regress $Y_t$ on $X_t$ by OLS
to get $\hat\beta$ (super-consistent: converges at rate $T$
rather than $\sqrt{T}$). (2) Test the residuals
$\hat e_t = Y_t - \hat\beta X_t$ for a unit root using ADF.
The critical values for this residual-based test differ
from the standard DF table (because $\hat\beta$ is estimated).

**Johansen's procedure** handles systems of $n$ potentially
cointegrated I(1) series. The rank of the long-run impact
matrix $\Pi$ in a VECM equals the number of cointegrating
vectors. Johansen's trace test and max-eigenvalue test are
likelihood ratio tests for the rank of $\Pi$; their
distributions are multivariate Brownian-motion functionals.
The full treatment belongs in [[`non-stationary-time-series.md`]].

## 9. Markov chains

### 9.1 Definitions

A discrete-time process $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$ with state space
$\mathcal{S}$ (countable or uncountable) is a **Markov chain**
if, for all $t$ and all states $A \subseteq \mathcal{S}$:
$$P(X_{t+1} \in A | X_0, X_1, \ldots, X_t) =
P(X_{t+1} \in A | X_t) \quad \text{a.s.}$$
The future depends on the present only — not the past. The
**transition kernel** $K(x, A) = P(X_{t+1} \in A | X_t = x)$
fully characterizes the chain's dynamics.

**In discrete state spaces:** the transition kernel is a
matrix $P \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{S}| \times |\mathcal{S}|}$
with rows summing to 1. The chain's distribution at time $n$
is $\mu_0 P^n$ where $\mu_0$ is the initial distribution.
VAR stability (spectral radius of the companion matrix,
[[`linear-algebra.md`]] §3.4) is the analogue for AR chains in
continuous state spaces.

### 9.2 Stationary distributions and ergodicity

A probability measure $\pi$ is a **stationary distribution**
if $\pi K = \pi$ (in the kernel sense: $\int \pi(dx)
K(x, A) = \pi(A)$). A stationary distribution is the
distribution the chain returns to when it has "forgotten"
its initial state.

**Conditions for existence and uniqueness:**
- *Irreducibility*: the chain can reach any state from any
  other state (in a finite number of steps).
- *Aperiodicity*: the chain is not forced to return to a
  state only in multiples of some period $> 1$.

For a finite irreducible aperiodic Markov chain: a unique
stationary distribution exists and $P^n \to \mathbf{1}\pi^\top$
geometrically fast (all rows converge to $\pi$).

**Ergodic theorem for Markov chains.** For an ergodic
(irreducible and aperiodic) Markov chain with stationary
distribution $\pi$ and initial distribution $\mu_0$:
$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1} f(X_t) \xrightarrow{a.s.} \int f\, d\pi$$
for every $\pi$-integrable $f$. Time averages converge to
ensemble averages — the Markov-chain analogue of §3.2.

### 9.3 Connections to dynamic programming

The contraction mapping theorem ([[`real-analysis.md`]] §6) and
the Blackwell sufficient conditions ([[`dynamic-programming.md`]])
establish that value function iteration converges for
discounted dynamic programming problems. The underlying
process is a Markov chain: the state variable $(X_t)$
evolves as a Markov chain under the optimal policy, and the
stationary distribution of that chain is the invariant
distribution the ergodic theorem applies to.

In **heterogeneous-agent models** (Bewley-Huggett-Aiyagari,
HANK): the individual state $(a_t, y_t)$ (assets, income)
evolves as a Markov chain; the distribution of agents
across states is the stationary distribution of this chain;
and general equilibrium clears markets at that stationary
distribution. The existence of a stationary distribution and
the speed of convergence to it (geometric for well-behaved
income processes) are Markov chain results. Numerical
computation of the stationary distribution uses value function
iteration on the Bellman equation and the Kolmogorov forward
equation (or Young's method for continuous states).

## 10. What this file doesn't cover, and where to find it

**Lévy processes.** Processes with stationary independent
increments that are not necessarily continuous (unlike Brownian
motion): Poisson processes, compound Poisson, stable processes.
Show up in jump-diffusion models of asset prices and in
extreme-event models. Not covered here.

**Empirical process theory.** Glivenko-Cantelli and Donsker
classes, VC dimension and covering numbers, bootstrap
consistency as a consequence of the functional CLT. These
tools are needed for the asymptotic theory of DML and
semiparametric estimators; they belong in a future [[`double-machine-learning.md`]]
section or a dedicated file. Van der Vaart and Wellner is
the reference.

**Point processes.** Duration models, counting processes,
the Cox model via the counting-process martingale framework
(Nelson-Aalen estimator). Not covered here.

**Kolmogorov forward and backward equations.** The PDEs
satisfied by the transition densities of diffusion processes.
These are the bridge between Itô calculus and structural
continuous-time macro (Bewley models in continuous time,
Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equations). The treatment belongs
in [[`dynamic-programming.md`]], which will cover the HJB
equation.

**The full Johansen trace test and VECM estimation.** The
detailed econometric implementation of cointegration
analysis — reduced rank regression, the VECM, lag selection,
deterministic terms, and the full distribution theory for
the trace and max-eigenvalue statistics — belongs in
[[`non-stationary-time-series.md`]].

**Fractional Brownian motion and long memory.** ARFIMA
processes, the Hurst exponent, and the self-similar processes
where short-memory approximations break down. These have
the flavor of non-stationary-time-series material more than
foundational math.
